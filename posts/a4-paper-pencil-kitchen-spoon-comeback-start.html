The Kitchen Spoon Tripod

How my art comeback actually started — with an A4 paper, a simple pencil, and whatever was in the kitchen drawer.

comeback, beginner, setup, day one

On April 17th, at some point in the afternoon, I decided I was done deciding.

I had been in decision mode for a long time. Should I get a proper sketchbook first? Should I buy better pencils? Should I wait until I had a tripod? The questions kept stacking and nothing was happening. Then something shifted — I realised none of it mattered. What mattered was whether I was willing to try. So I stopped waiting and started.

What I had

One sheet of A4 printing paper. The kind that comes out of office printers. A single HB pencil from a drawer. An iPhone 13 with no mount, no clamp, no tripod of any kind.

The filming problem took about ten minutes to solve. I needed the phone overhead, stable, pointing down at the paper. I found a kitchen spoon, tied it to the phone with a cotton rope, and balanced the whole thing against whatever was nearby. It looked ridiculous. It worked perfectly.

Why an eye first

Most artists returning to drawing start with an eye. There is a reason for this — the eye is the most emotionally loaded feature on a human face. If you can draw a convincing eye, you feel like you can draw. It is the fastest path from blank paper to something that looks real.

I did warmup exercises first. Basic strokes, pressure control, trying to remember how the pencil felt in my hand after 10+ years of not using one. The muscle memory was buried somewhere. The warmup was the excavation.

What it actually felt like

Strange. The eye that came out was not wrong exactly — the proportions were roughly correct — but everything felt uncertain. The shadows were weak. I had no confidence in how dark to go, when to stop, whether the shading was reading as depth or just as random marks. The session ran 30 to 40 minutes and I kept going because I genuinely did not know where the stopping point was.

That uncertainty is the honest thing about coming back after a long time. The knowledge is still there but the trust in your own judgment has to be rebuilt from scratch.

What I learned on day one

You do not need the right equipment. You need the decision to start. A4 paper draws the same as a Brustro sketchbook on day one. A kitchen spoon holds a phone as well as a ₹3,000 overhead clamp on day one. What matters on day one is that it happened — not how it looked.
