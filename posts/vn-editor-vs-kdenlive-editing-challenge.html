I Opened Kdenlive and Felt Like I Was in a Jungle

VN editor worked. I wanted more. Kdenlive scared me. Here is what I learned about choosing the right editing tool when you are just starting out.

editing, tools, process, shorts

VN editor was working. My Shorts were getting published. The editing was simple — trim, speed up, add audio, export. That was enough for the first few weeks.

Then I started wanting more. Specifically the Ken Burns effect — a slow zoom or pan across the sketch that gives still images a cinematic feel. VN didn't have it. So I did what anyone does: I searched online and almost every result said the same thing. Use Kdenlive.

Opening Kdenlive for the first time

I downloaded it. I opened it. And I froze.

Every panel had buttons I didn't recognise. There were timelines, tracks, effects libraries, transition menus, keyframe editors. I had never done desktop video editing before — my entire experience was VN on iPhone. This was a completely different world.

The feeling I remember most is fear. Not of the software — of accidentally breaking something. What if I touched the wrong button and deleted all the footage? What if I misunderstood what a track was and corrupted the file? I didn't know enough to know what was safe to click and what wasn't. So I didn't click anything. I just stared at it for a while and closed the window.

Why I went back to VN

I am making Shorts. Short videos — 30 to 60 seconds — filmed on a phone, edited on a phone. The content is graphite pencil on paper. There is no dialogue, no complex cuts, no colour grading. The editing needs are minimal: remove dead frames, speed up the slow sections, add a background track, export vertical.

Kdenlive is a professional non-linear video editor built for complex projects. I was using a sledgehammer to tap a nail. The mismatch between what I needed and what Kdenlive offered was the actual problem — not my skill level.

VN does everything my Shorts need. It is fast, intuitive, and designed for exactly the format I am working in. The Ken Burns effect can wait until I have a reason that justifies the learning curve.

VN editor — mobile, fast, designed for Shorts. Trim, speed ramp, audio, text overlay. Does everything OviyamArt needs right now.

Kdenlive — desktop, professional, powerful. Ken Burns, keyframes, multi-track. Too complex for current needs. Worth learning when long-form editing begins.

Instagram Edits — new addition. Still testing. Early impression is positive — built specifically for Reels format with good audio sync tools.

What I am using now

VN for Shorts. Instagram Edits for Reels — I have only just started using it so I cannot give a full comparison yet. But the early experience is that it understands the vertical format natively in a way that desktop editors simply don't.

The lesson from the Kdenlive experiment: choose the tool that matches the format you are working in, not the tool that has the most features. More features means more decisions. More decisions means more paralysis. At the beginning of a comeback — when the goal is to keep drawing and keep publishing — the simpler tool is almost always the right tool.

Kdenlive is still installed. I will come back to it when I start editing long-form videos with multiple sections, voiceover, and Ken Burns transitions across the sketch. That day will come. It just isn't today.
